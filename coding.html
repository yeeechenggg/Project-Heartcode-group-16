WHY IS CYBERBULLYING SO SERIOUS?

People have almost constant access to their devices, which makes cyberbullying hard to escape and make them feel like they never get a break. Hence, the effects of cyberbullying can be felt very strongly.

Cyberbullying can also result in long-lasting emotional impacts, even after the bullying has stopped. As it is done anonymously, cyberbullying is hard to both trace and prevent. The victim also has no idea how many people have seen the derogatory messages or posts.


HOW DOES CYBERBULLYING AFFECT THE MENTAL HEALTH OF VICTIMS?

  • Victims may have increased stress and lowered self-esteem.
  • They may develop mental health issues such as anxiety and depression and other stress related disorders.
  • They may act out violently.
  • They may have difficulty focusing and concentrating which affects their performance at school or at work.
  • In some cases, some victims have attempted to commit suicide.

THERE ARE MANY DIFFERENT TYPES OF CYBERBULLYING, BUT THESE ARE SOME OF THE MOST COMMON ONES:


Denigration

Posting information online that is untrue and damaging about another.

This diminishes the victim's standing with others, severing supportive ties and turning others against them. It makes the victim feel worthless and unworthy, crushing their confidence.

Cyberstalking

Using electronic communications to stalk a victim.

Some examples include, but are not limited to: Sending unwanted, frightening, or obscene emails and text messages, tracking the victim's computer and internet use.

This causes the victim to experience extreme psychological distress and they may develop depression, isolation, anxiety and become extremely cautious of their surroundings.

Impersonating/Masquerading

Posting offensive or aggressive messages under another person's name online.

This has lasting impacts on the person's reputation and image both online and in real life.

Flaming

Posting personal insults and vulgar and angry words

Common characteristics of flaming includes targeting personal details of a person, such as their race, gender, sexuality, economic status, or appearance, regardless of whether those things are relevant to the topic.

Flaming is an intense argument that takes place in chatrooms, via instant messages or email or in the comments section of social-media sites and YouTube.
